How Researchers Read and Evaluate Papers

Before we begin

This is a study about how researchers and research trainees evaluate scientific claims and reason about evidence. The study also includes questions about AI assistants, meaning conversational tools like ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and similar products that you can ask questions or give tasks to in plain language.

The study takes approximately 1 hour.

Consent Form

Purpose of the study. This study is being conducted by Alison Gopnik, professor of psychology at the University of California, Berkeley, and her laboratory of research assistants, graduate students, and post-doctoral fellows. We invite you to participate in a study about how researchers and research trainees read, understand, and evaluate scientific papers. Participation is entirely voluntary.

Study procedures. There will be one testing session that will last approximately 1 hour. You will be asked to complete a short survey about your background and your experience with AI assistants. You will then read three short research papers and answer questions about each one. Some participants may be provided with access to an AI assistant while completing the task. If you are provided with access to an AI assistant, your interactions with the assistant may be recorded as part of the study data.

Some questions will ask for your own interpretations, evaluations, or opinions. There are no right or wrong answers to these questions. Please answer based on your own understanding and judgment. You may refrain from answering any questions that make you uncomfortable and may withdraw your participation at any time, even after completing the study, without penalty.

Benefits. There is no direct benefit to you, but you may find the task interesting. You will also be making an important contribution to improving our understanding of how people evaluate research materials.

Risks and discomforts. We do not expect you to face any foreseeable risks from your participation. There is the unlikely possibility that your confidentiality will be compromised; however, we have taken measures to minimize this risk.

Confidentiality. Your identity will be separated from your data, and a random numerical code will be assigned to keep track of the data. Your confidentiality will be kept to the degree permitted by the technology being used. No guarantees can be made regarding the interception of data sent via the Internet by any third parties.

Data will be kept indefinitely. All information will be securely stored at the University in encrypted format. Only laboratory personnel will have access to this data. No individuals will be identified in any presentation or publication.

Identifiers may be removed from identifiable private information. After such removal, the information could be used for future research studies or distributed to other investigators for future research studies without additional informed consent from you.

Compensation. You will receive compensation as described in the study listing. This study contains a number of checks to make sure that participants are completing the tasks honestly and carefully. As long as you read the instructions and complete the tasks, your submission will be approved. If you fail these checks, your submission may be rejected and you may not be compensated.

Rights. Your participation in the research is voluntary, and you are free to withdraw your consent and discontinue participation at any time. Whether or not you participate in this study will have no bearing on your status with respect to any program, department, or service at the University of California, and will involve no adverse consequences.

Questions. If you have any questions or concerns, please contact the study team. If you have any questions regarding your rights as a participant in this research project, please contact the Committee for the Protection of Human Subjects at the University of California, Berkeley at (510) 642-7461 or subjects@berkeley.edu.

Debrief

The purpose of this study is to understand how people evaluate research claims, reason about evidence, and, for some participants, use AI tools during scientific reasoning tasks. The three research summaries you read describe genuine analyses conducted for this study; they were not drawn from previously published literature, so please do not treat their specific statistics as established findings to cite or rely on for real-world decisions.
