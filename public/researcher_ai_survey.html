How Researchers Read and Evaluate Papers

Before we begin

This is a study about how researchers and research trainees evaluate scientific claims and reason about evidence. The study also includes questions about AI assistants, meaning conversational tools such as ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, and similar products that you can ask questions or give tasks to in plain language.

The study takes approximately 45-60 minutes.

Required to proceed.
Have you heard of and used at least one of the AI assistants described above?

Consent Form

Purpose of the study. This study is being conducted by Alison Gopnik, professor of psychology at the University of California, Berkeley, and her laboratory of research assistants, graduate students, and postdoctoral fellows. We invite you to participate in a study about how researchers and research trainees read, understand, and evaluate scientific papers. Participation is entirely voluntary.

Study procedures. There will be one testing session lasting approximately 45-60 minutes. You will first complete questions about your background, your usual approach to research work, and your experience with AI assistants. You will then read and evaluate two short research studies. Some participants will be provided with access to an AI assistant while completing the paper evaluation task. If you are provided with access to an AI assistant, your interactions with it will be recorded as part of the study data.

Some questions will ask for your interpretations, evaluations, or opinions. There are no right or wrong answers to those questions. Please answer based on your own understanding and judgment. You may refrain from answering any question that makes you uncomfortable and may withdraw from the study at any time without penalty.

Benefits. There is no direct benefit to you, but you may find the task interesting. You will also contribute to improving our understanding of how people evaluate research materials.

Risks and discomforts. We do not expect you to face any foreseeable risks beyond those ordinarily encountered when completing an online survey. There is a small possibility that confidentiality could be compromised; however, we have taken measures to minimize this risk.

Confidentiality. Your identity will be separated from your research data, and a numerical code will be used to organize the data. Your confidentiality will be protected to the degree permitted by the technology being used. No guarantees can be made regarding the interception of data sent over the Internet by third parties.

Data storage and future use. Data will be stored securely at the University in encrypted format and may be retained indefinitely. Only authorized laboratory personnel will have access to identifiable study records. No individual will be identified in a presentation or publication. After identifiers are removed, the de-identified information may be used for future research or shared with other investigators without additional consent.

Compensation. You will receive compensation as described in the study listing. The study includes checks intended to determine whether participants are completing the tasks honestly and carefully. As long as you read the instructions and make a good-faith effort to complete the study, your submission will be approved. Submissions may be rejected if there is clear evidence that the study was not completed as instructed.

Rights. Your participation is voluntary. You may withdraw your consent and discontinue participation at any time. Whether or not you participate will have no bearing on your status with respect to any University of California program, department, or service.

Questions. For questions about the study, contact the research team. For questions about your rights as a research participant, contact the Committee for the Protection of Human Subjects at the University of California, Berkeley at (510) 642-7461 or subjects@berkeley.edu.

Debrief

The purpose of this study is to understand how people evaluate research claims, reason about evidence, and, for some participants, with or without AI tools during scientific reasoning tasks. To study these processes fairly, we did not want participants' responses to depend on prior knowledge of a real topic, article, or research area. For that reason, the research articles you read were artificially constructed for this study and were not real published research papers. The findings, statistics, citations, and implications in the articles were included as part of the study materials and should not be treated as established scientific evidence or as advice for real-world decisions.

Thank you very much for participating!
